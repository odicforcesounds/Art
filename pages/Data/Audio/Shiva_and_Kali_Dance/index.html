Shiva and Kali moved across the edge of the universe, their bodies writing the first poetry of existence. He stepped with absolute Discipline, his feet grounding the trembling stars, while she spun wildly, the bone Skeleton at her hips clicking like a frantic metronome. Every time she would Fly into his arms, galaxies bloomed from the heat of their embrace. It was their first true Contact, a passionate exploration of order and chaos surrendering to one another. Yet, perched on a nebulous cloud just below their cosmic ballroom, the Monkey paid them no mind. He was consumed by deep Concentration, hunched over a glowing terminal woven from stardust and mathematical threads. He was obsessed with building a game for mortal society, a vast simulation he believed would give them purpose. Sparks of yellow energy crackled from his fingertips as he frantically coded the base mechanics, pulling physics from the dark underwater trenches and stretching them to the highest celestial boundaries. "Do not Deny the rhythm," Kali laughed, her dark eyes flashing as she pulled Shiva closer, tasting the wildness of creation on his breath. He answered with a smile, pulling forth a golden Sax from the ether. He blew a deep, resonant note that vibrated through the foundation of the void, melting her destructive wrath into a fierce, gravitational attraction. Their bodies moved in perfect symmetry, an eternal healing for the ancient wounds of the cosmos. Below, the Monkey frantically applied a correction to the societal loops he was designing. He watched his virtual mortals ride along a ghost circuit of endless, hollow routines. He was entirely connected to his creation, blind to the divine romance swirling above his head. Whenever there was a delay in the rendering of his world, his neurotic mind raced, tweaking the variables, desperate to perfect the karma algorithm he had implemented to judge his tiny, simulated humans. "You lose your focus?" Kali teased Shiva, noticing his third eye drifting downward to watch the frantic primate. "He builds a cage and calls it an empire," Shiva murmured, catching her by the waist and dipping her into the nebula. The Monkey didn't hear them. He was too busy defining the terms of his grand simulation, establishing the strict rules of survival for the society he thought he was saving. He believed he was the architect of reality. He didn't realize that his terminal, his code, his frantic labor, and the very society he was gamifying were all just a dream fleeting, beautiful illusion generated entirely by the warmth of the two gods dancing above him in the dark.
